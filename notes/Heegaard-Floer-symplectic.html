Bordered Floer Homology and Fukaya Category

In this talk I'll some motivations and backgrounds of my recent joint work in progress with Suk-joo Lee, Yin Li and Cheuk-Yu Mak. Given a $3$-manifold $Y$ with a decomposition $Y=Y_1\cup_{\partial} Y_2$, by an idea of Lipschitz-Ozsváth-Thurston [LOT18], we can associate to such a decomposition a triple $(\mathcal{A} ,HFD(Y_1),HFA(Y_2))$ where $\mathcal{A}$ is a dg algebra, $HFD(Y_1)$ is a dg $\mathcal{A}$-module, and $HFA(Y_2)$ is an $\mathcal{A}_{\infty}$-module.

Recall that for any compact $3$-manifold $Y$ without boundary, we can describe $Y$ using Morse theory: pick a Morse-Smale function $f\colon Y\to\mathbb{R}$ with a unique minimum and a unique maximum, and suppose further that the index $i$ critical points of $f$ lie in $f^{-1} (i)$. Let $\Sigma=f^{-1} (3/2)$, then $\Sigma$ is a closed surface, which intersects transversely with the stable manifolds of index $1$ critical points and the unstable manifolds of index $2$ critical points. We call the previous intersections $\alpha$-curves, and the latter intersections $\beta$-curves. They're cycles on $\Sigma$ generating $H_1 (\Sigma;\mathbb{Z})$. The Riemann surface $\Sigma$, together with the $\alpha$- and $\beta$-curves, then determine up to diffeomorphism the topological type of $Y$, and we call this triple $(\Sigma ,\boldsymbol{\alpha} ,\boldsymbol{\beta})$ a Heegaard diagram. Fix a point $z\in\Sigma$ which is not in the $\alpha$- and $\beta$-curves, then the Heegaard-Floer homology is a Floer-type homology theory associated to such a pointed Heegaard diagram, generated by pairs of intersection points of the $\alpha$- and $\beta$-curves, and differential given by counting holomorphic curves.

A Pointed Heegaard Diagram

In the bordered case, we can imagine cutting the three-manifold $Y$ "vertically", so that it cuts the Riemann surface $\Sigma$ along a circle $Z$. The resulting manifold-with-boundary $Y_1$ and $Y_2$ then have bordered Heegaard diagram whose underlying surface is a surface with one boundary component $\Sigma_i$, the $\beta$-curves of $Y$ are divided into two classes $\boldsymbol{\beta}_1$ in $\Sigma_1$ and $\boldsymbol{\beta}_2$ in $\Sigma_2$. Some $\alpha$-curves are cut along the circle, so the resulting $\alpha$-curves on $\Sigma_1$ consisting of two types: if the genus of $\Sigma_1$ is $g$, then we have $g-k$ $\alpha$-circles in the interior of $\Sigma_1$, for some $k$, and $2k$ $\alpha$-arcs with endpoints on the boundary circle $Z$. For simplicity, we also require that the base point lies also on $Z$. Now the bordered Heegaard diagram for $Y_1$ is a triple $(\Sigma_1 ,\boldsymbol{\alpha}_1 ,\boldsymbol{\beta}_1)$, and similarly for $Y_2$.
